How one author is hoping Taylor Swift will bring more women into economics

Bryan West   USA TODAY NETWORK

Economist and author Misty Heggeness has a clear mission behind her new book "Swiftynomics": make economics more engaging, more accessible and more inviting for girls and women.

"I want more women to go into the field of economics," she said over Zoom. "I want more women to see themselves as economic agents."

The Overland Park, Kansas, author first felt the spark to write during the pandemic, when she was a research economist at the United States Census Bureau. As she began producing her own statistics on mothers' labor force participation, she was surprised at how much she enjoyed translating economics for a wider audience. At the same time, she found herself inspired by Taylor Swift's creative surge for her "Folklore" and "Evermore" albums.

"I had been paying much closer attention to her career since 2019 when I saw the music video for 'The Man,'" she said.

By early 2022, she knew Swift would be the "muse" through whom she could tell a bigger story about modern women in the economy.

Her writing process unfolded slowly but joyfully. After signing with the University of California Press in summer 2022, she spent nights, Saturday mornings and Sunday mornings drafting chapters while her teenagers slept in. She didn't retreat to a fellowship or take leave from teaching at the University of Kansas. She simply wrote whenever life allowed.

"Writing this book gave me so much joy," she said. "I love interpreting. I want more women to find a space for themselves in either studying economics or thinking like an economist."

That same mission now fuels her class at Kansas, "The Economics of Taylor Swift."

The gender split stunned even her.

"I've got three young men in my class and the rest are young women," she said. Yet when her class clears out and the next, Introduction to Microeconomics, files in, "it's three young ladies and the rest are young men."

For Heggeness, the contrast proves her point: If economics were taught in a way that met young women where they were, more would stay.

The book may draw inspiration from the Eras Tour mastermind, but it's geared toward her fandom of Swifties. Heggeness titled it "Swiftynomics" — joking it should "probably be Swiftienomics, but the 'ie' didn't initially look as nice."

When asked what she'd tell Swift if given the chance, Heggeness didn’t hesitate.

"I would honestly just want to tell her how proud I am of her," she said. "I'm so happy that people like her exist."

To order "Swiftynomics: How Women Mastermind and Redefine Our Economy," go to swiftynomics.com.
